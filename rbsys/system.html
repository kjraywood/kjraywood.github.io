System Overview

1. General Approach

 • 1NT = 15 – 17
 • Mexican 2♦︎ = balanced 18 – 19
 • 2NT = 20 – bad 22
 • Strong 2♣︎
 • Five-card majors
 • semi-forcing 1NT
 • 2/1 GF
 • aggressive initial actions

1.1. Responses to minor opening

  • responding to 1♣︎: criteria for bypassing diamonds based on anticpating the auction

  • inverted minors by unPH, single-raise = GF, jump-raise = mixed, 2♠︎ = LR

  • traditional NT ranges:  2NT = 13-15 or 18+ 
    ➤  opener shows shortness,   except 1♦︎-2NT; 3♣︎ = natural 2nd-suit

  • 2♥︎ = balanced 11–12  ➤  2♠︎ ➜ 2NT

  • jump-shift other-minor = natural inv, traditional follow-ups after one-suited inv.

1.2. Responses to major opening

  • 3/1 = natural inv., traditional one-suit-inv follow-ups

  • 1♥︎–2♠︎ ∼ Jacoby 2NT  ➤  swap ♠︎ and NT @ all levels

  • 1♠︎–3NT & 1♥︎–3♠︎ = weak splinter

  • limit raises  ➤  follow-ups ∼ game-try after single raise

  • Nebulous Two Clubs 
    ➤  next-step below 2NT = nebulous
      ⇒  we get up to two nebulous bids each

  • Schuler Shift after 1M–2♦︎ or 1♠︎–2♥︎

3.3.1. When opener passes

Responder can redouble as re‑Stayman, but must have at least two clubs and at least invitational values because opener might have a stack and be intending to pass.

With a garbage-Stayman hand (three-suiter short in clubs), responder bids 2♦︎ and opener corrects with a four-card major.

With short-clubs and a GF, responder bids 3♣︎ as re‑Stayman. Smolen is ON.

Other bids are as though opener had replied to Stayman with 2♦︎, although in this case, opener may still have a four-card major. It’s possible to miss a 4‑-4 major-suit fit when responder rebids 2NT and opener passes.

53.1. The aggressive shift

We give attitude on opening lead, even in a suit-contract with shortness (s|v) in dummy. A discouraging signal implies that we can stand the aggressive shift; usually to dummy’s weakest side-suit.

We identify the aggressive shift with the following rules in order of priority.

53.1.1. Negatives

not trumps

not a suit headed by AKQ, KQJ, or AKJT

not a singleton or void in a suit-contract except it could be a singleton vs a slam

not a suit that was shown by declarer

53.1.2. Positives

opening-leader’s suit - when a different suit was led

suit shown by leader’s partner - when a different suit was led

vs suit:
 • a 3-card suit headed by at most   one honour
 • otherwise shortest side-suit   (but not s|v below slam)

vs notrump:
 • shortest side-suit not excluded by   a negative

53.1.3. Problem hands

In third seat when you don’t want the suit led, and you don’t want the aggressive shift, signal for the suit that you think is least likely to cost a trick.

 An unusual card requests the unusual (non-obvious) shift

53.4. Following suit from honour sequences

  Following suit from honour sequences    –  Ed Zaluski

53.4.1. Third-hand play

When partner leads low and we have an honour sequence, we usually play 2nd-highest .

2nd highest reveals declarer’s high honours to partner.

However, if we know that lowest of the sequence will make our holding known to partner, then that’s the card we play, unless we are trying to disguise our holding from declarer.

53.4.2. Second-hand play

If choosing to split:
 • split low from a two-card sequence
 • split high from a three-card sequence   (or equiv.)

Always splitting high is standard, but the above scheme is usually readable and helps partner know if an active defence is needed. See Zaluski for details.

54. Discards

  • Upside-down attitude unless it is in dummy’s long-suit and partner needs count

  • when our first discard in a suit is attitude, our subsequent discards in that suit are either suit-preference (first priority) or count

54.1. General

If declarer has shown out of a suit, discards of that suit are suit-preference.

We discard immediately from a suit we cannot guard; unless it is declarer’s guess suit.

54.2. Discards vs NoTrump

If we lead a suit against NT, declarer wins and runs a suit forcing discards from us, including discards from the suit we have led, then:
 • an early discard from our led suit   means that our suit is not ready to run
 • a late discard from our led suit   means that our suit is ready to run

At notrump, an encouraging attitude-discard tends to show either:
 • a stopper so that partner can discard   that suit and save stoppers in other suits
  -OR-
 • a quick entry so that you can win a trick   and lead partner’s long-suit through declarer

The appearence of dummy plus the play usually makes the intention clear.

55. Special Defence Situations

55.1. Getting a Ruff

When you intend to shift to a singleton and hold AKx in the led suit, win the first trick with the ace.

By making it appear that declarer has the king, you discourage other lines of defence.

55.2. Suit-Preference Returns

When playing a suit for partner to ruff and dummy holds no honour-card:
 • Q or T = high-suit pref.  • J = low-suit pref.

This generally applies when it was not possible to signal suit preference when winning the suit. e.g.  winning the Ace and returning through declarer’s King.

55.3. Suit-preference by third hand with AKQ

The first card shows suit preference

If the first card is subsequently known to be the middle-card then the message is no suit-preference. This usually means nothing useful outside, but could mean a secondary honour, usually the king, in both side suits if that makes more sense on the bidding.

Playing the second and third card in the the same order as the first and second reinforces the preference shown by the first card. i.e.  strong preference.

Playing the second and third card in the opposite order means that the preference shown is mild.
